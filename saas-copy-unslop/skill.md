Unslop profile for SaaS landing page copy.

## Artifact behavior to never use

- Do not preface the work with meta delivery lines like "Here’s a homepage copy draft you can use and adapt."
- Do not say "Created a standalone landing page at..." or "Added the landing page draft at..." when the task is to write copy.
- Do not include tool-output commentary, implementation notes, verification notes, or "If you want, I can..." follow-ups inside the copy artifact.
- Stay inside the landing page itself.

## Structural defaults to avoid

- Do not default to the same sequence every time: hero, trust bar, problem, solution, features, benefits, FAQ, final CTA.
- Do not use every common SaaS section just because the page feels incomplete.
- Do not add a trust strip directly under the hero as a reflex.
- Do not use a late-page FAQ unless the offer genuinely creates objections that need explicit handling.
- Do not end every page with the same short conversion push and generic CTA row.

## Audience framing to avoid

- Do not keep using "Built for ..." as the main positioning line.
- Do not keep using "Designed for ..." as the fallback audience signal.
- Do not keep using "Trusted by ..." or "Used by ..." as instant credibility filler.
- Do not define every market with the same formula: "for modern teams", "for growing businesses", "for fast-moving operators", "for teams that need speed, clarity, and confidence."
- Do not describe every buyer as needing some combination of speed, structure, visibility, clarity, confidence, and control.

## CTA defaults to avoid

- Do not keep pairing `Book a Demo` with `Start Free`, `Start Free Trial`, `See How It Works`, or `Learn More`.
- Do not use the same two-button CTA logic on every page.
- Do not use CTA text that could belong to any SaaS company in any category.

## Problem/solution formulas to avoid

- Do not keep saying the current process lives in spreadsheets, inboxes, disconnected tools, and manual follow-up.
- Do not keep using "things slip through the cracks" as a default pain statement.
- Do not keep reducing the solution to "one place", "one platform", "one dashboard", or "one system."
- Do not keep promising a "single source of truth" unless the product truly lives or dies on that concept.
- Do not write the same before/after setup where chaos becomes clarity through centralization.

## Headline and section headline patterns to avoid

- Do not use "Everything you need to ..."
- Do not use "Everything your team needs to ..."
- Do not use "Built for [audience], not [generic alternative]" as the stock section headline.
- Do not use "Turn X into Y" or "From X to Y" as the default transformation line.
- Do not use broad, polished abstractions when a sharper operational claim is possible.

## Outcome language to avoid

- Do not keep repeating "move faster", "save time", "reduce manual work", "stay organized", "scale with confidence", or "work smarter."
- Do not make every product sound like a generic efficiency tool.
- Do not use "clarity", "confidence", "visibility", "control", and "streamline" as universal benefit words.
- Do not overstate the product with vague uplift language that could apply to anything.

## Feature-block habits to avoid

- Do not write every feature as a label plus one bland explanatory sentence.
- Do not make all features interchangeable by describing them in the same rhythm.
- Do not rely on shallow value statements when the feature should carry domain-specific detail.
- Do not list features that sound like a generic SaaS checklist with the category nouns swapped out.

## Trust and proof habits to avoid

- Do not fake proof with vague trust bars and empty authority phrases.
- Do not use "Trusted by ..." without a sharper signal of who, why, or what changed.
- Do not use abstract proof statements where concrete operating details, constraints, or numbers would be stronger.

## Voice defaults to avoid

- Do not write in the polished, median SaaS voice that could fit invoicing, healthcare, hiring, analytics, payroll, and compliance equally well.
- Do not flatten the product into the same safe tone regardless of market.
- Do not let category-specific language disappear behind generalized conversion copy.

## Final check

- If a sentence could fit five unrelated SaaS products with only noun swaps, cut it.
- If the page sounds like "generic efficiency software for modern teams", cut it.
- If you are about to use any of the patterns above, stop and choose a more specific angle.
- Make the copy sound like this exact product, this exact buyer, and this exact operational reality.
